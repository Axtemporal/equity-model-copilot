# The Compressed (Tech) Life Cycle

**What it is.** The compressed life cycle is the "aging in dog years" pattern of successful technology firms — a taller, narrower curve with a steep rise, a brief mature plateau, an abrupt decline, and near-zero salvage value — in contrast to the long, comfortable maturity of an industrial firm.

## Core idea

Technology has compressed the [[corporate-lifecycle]]. Where a classic industrial firm grows slowly and then holds its peak for a long mature stretch, a successful tech firm scales up explosively, plateaus only briefly, and then collapses faster and harder than the analyst expects. Four structural reasons drive it:

1. **Scaling is easy** — low entry barriers and low upfront capital fuel explosive growth, but the same openness invites competitors.
2. **Maturity is brief** — tech's competitive advantages are *fleeting* and deplete quickly, unlike the durable brand/scale [[moat|moats]] of consumer and industrial firms, so the plateau is short.
3. **Decline is rapid** — the very forces that enable fast growth (open entry, low switching costs, easy scaling) also accelerate the collapse, and it is hard to reverse.
4. **Liquidation value is minimal** — asset-light tech has little tangible substance to fall back on once earnings power erodes, so the salvage floor is near zero.

In the language of [[lifecycle-determinants]], the compressed curve maximises height and steepness while sacrificing length and flatness. A stylised contrast: tech grows ~50% for ~5 years, holds a ~5-year mature plateau at ~10% growth, then declines rapidly; a non-tech firm grows more gently (~25%) but holds maturity for ~10 years.

## Across the life cycle

The compression bends every stage of the curve relative to the industrial baseline:

- **Climb:** faster and higher — a steeper steepness, an earlier and taller peak.
- **Plateau:** short — the [[moat]] is narrow and time-limited, so margins and ROIC fade fast.
- **Decline:** abrupt and largely irreversible.
- **End game:** almost no salvage — the [[liquidation-value]] floor sits near zero, so a deeply distressed asset-light firm cannot lean on an asset-sale value.

Because the arc runs faster, **young tech looks expensive on P/E even when fairly valued**, while **old tech (>35 years) looks artificially cheap** and trades at lower multiples than old non-tech. Old tech rationally returns cash via buybacks rather than dividends, anticipating its own short remaining runway ([[cash-return-dividends-buybacks]]).

## Mechanics / formulas

The compression is implemented in a model by *reshaping the standard inputs*, not by a new method:

- **Shorten the mature plateau** — hold high margins/growth for fewer years before fading.
- **Steepen the decline** — let revenue and margins roll over faster than for an industrial comparable.
- **Set the salvage floor near zero** — asset-light firms have little [[liquidation-value]].
- **Prefer finite-life or negative-terminal-growth models** — a naïve perpetual-growth [[terminal-value]] systematically *over-values* fast-aging tech; consider a finite growing annuity or a low/negative terminal growth rate instead.
- **Weaken mean reversion** — do not assume high margins revert to historical highs; under disruption a decline may be structural ([[measuring-lifecycle-stage]]).

The single biggest valuation error this guards against: transplanting an industrial firm's long, gentle maturity onto a tech firm, which inflates the [[terminal-value]] piece.

## Pitfalls & nuances

- **PEG can flash "undervalued" exactly when growth is least sustainable.** A low [[valuation-multiples|PEG]] on explosive but fleeting growth is a trap for growth investors ([[growth-investing]]).
- **Classic value tenets misfire on tech** — trusting P/E, "buy good companies forever," and preferring dividends to buybacks all break when the cycle is compressed.
- **Disruption risk is now broad, not tech-only.** The probability of being disrupted has risen for almost every business, so the compression mindset increasingly applies beyond pure tech — and even moated incumbents need contingency plans.
- **The holding-company escape:** blending many sub-cycles ([[sum-of-the-parts-octopus]]) can offset any single compressed business's short arc.
