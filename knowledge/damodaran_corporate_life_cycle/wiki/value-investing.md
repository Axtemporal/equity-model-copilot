# Value Investing

**What it is.** Value investing is the philosophy purpose-built for mature firms: its edge is valuing *assets in place*, and it seeks companies trading below what their existing earnings and cash flows are worth — distinct from paying large premiums for future growth.

> Compliance note: this article describes value investing analytically — its strands, evidence and critiques. Nothing here is a recommendation.

## Core idea
Damodaran defines a value investor not as anyone who wants to buy cheap (that would catch growth investors too) but specifically as someone whose comparative advantage is assessing investments a firm has *already made*, and who is leery of large premiums for growth. That is why value investing gravitates to **mature** companies, where value is dominated by assets in place. The chapter splits the philosophy into three strands:

- **The Screeners (passive value).** Belief: mature, boring, predictable firms with stable earnings and strong cash flows are systematically underpriced because investors ignore them. Strategy: run quantitative filters (and some qualitative judgment) and buy what passes. The tradition traces to Ben Graham.
- **The Contrarians.** Belief: mature firms are roughly fairly priced on average but get mis-valued after big information events because markets *overreact*. Strategy: buy the names the market has overpunished and wait for the overreaction to unwind. The deep end is vulture / distressed investing.
- **The Buy-and-Hold (quality / Buffett).** Belief: genuinely good businesses run by superior managers outperform over the long run. Strategy: buy quality at a fair price and hold for the very long run.

Raw low-PE/low-PBV screening is the *floor*, not the philosophy — the real claim is that the analysis layered on top (durable moats, good management) generates the payoff. The honest benchmark is therefore not whether cheap stocks beat the market on paper, but whether an active value picker beats a **value index fund** already tilted to low price-to-book.

## What makes a company "good"
For the quality strand, "good" is defined precisely: **not** raw profitability and **not** growth (which can be value-neutral or even value-destroying), but a **high excess return** — [[roic-and-excess-returns|return on invested capital well above the cost of capital]], sustained by a durable [[moat]]. This is the same quality lens used to value mature firms in [[valuing-mature]] and the formal version of Buffett's "wonderful business" language.

## Across the life cycle
Value investing is the **mature-stage** counterpart to [[growth-investing]] (youth) — the two map onto opposite ends of the value-vs-growth balance-sheet split. Contrarian plays cluster on mature firms hit by bad news and slide toward decline, overlapping with [[activist-investing]] and the old-age toolkit of [[private-equity-and-lbo]]. The performance of value vs. growth is itself cyclical, tied to the earnings cycle and the macro/rate environment.

## Mechanics / formulas
- **Excess return (quality test):** ROIC − cost of capital; a "good company" has ROIC ≫ cost of capital, sustained by a moat.
- **Justified-PE intuition:** from a stable-growth dividend model, PE = Payout / (cost of equity − g). Higher growth and higher ROE raise the justified PE; higher risk lowers it. A stock is "cheap" only if it trades *below* its justified PE given its fundamentals — not merely because its absolute PE is low. (See [[valuation-multiples]] for the full derivation.)
- **The "cheap vs. expensive" cheat-sheet:** a low multiple is genuinely cheap only when the matching value driver does *not* justify it — ROE for PBV, ROIC for EV/Invested Capital, growth/risk/payout for PE.
- **Graham's classic screens** (illustrative): earnings yield ≥ 2× the AAA bond yield; price < ⅔ of tangible book; price < ⅔ of net current asset value; conservative leverage and current-ratio tests; positive long-run EPS growth with few declining years.
- **Margin of safety (MOS)** is a *trade-off*, not a creed: it should rise with how imprecise the value estimate is and how hard the stock is to re-buy. Excessive MOS just parks the portfolio in cash, and MOS operates only *after* a valuation that still needs its own explicit risk input.

## Pitfalls & nuances
- **The lost decade.** Over 2010–2019, growth beat value in seven of ten years and low-PBV stocks badly trailed high-PBV stocks. Four explanations are offered: a passing cyclical phase; the Fed (QE subsidizing risk-taking); a "flatter" world where cheap data eroded the screening moat; and a globalized, platform-built economy that made balance-sheet nostrums obsolete. Damodaran leans toward the structural explanations over the comforting cyclical one.
- **The "three R's" critique.** Value investing decayed into something like a religion — *rigid* (a dividend obsession that herded investors into yesterday's economy just as firms shifted to buybacks), *ritualistic* (uncritical veneration of Graham and Buffett), and *righteous* (a sense of entitlement to high returns and contempt for other investors).
- **The historical premium has two readings.** Cheap stocks may have out-returned the market because low-PBV proxies for *missed risk* (Fama–French), in which case it is no free lunch — or because of *market inefficiency*, in which case it is real alpha. The premium's collapse reopened this debate.
- **The prescription to reinvent it:** separate value from price; embrace rather than flee uncertainty (use distributions, decision trees, simulation); trust accounting numbers less; treat MOS as a trade-off; combine stock-picking with diversification (~30–40 ideas, since both the value estimate and the timing of convergence are uncertain); and respect other philosophies. Most importantly for mature firms, turn to **activist value investing** — taking a large stake to *force* the change that unlocks trapped value.
