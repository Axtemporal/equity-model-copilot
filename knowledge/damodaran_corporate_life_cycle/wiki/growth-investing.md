# Growth Investing

**What it is.** Growth investing is the philosophy aimed at the young, high-growth end of the life cycle: its edge is valuing *growth assets* — the value of investments a firm has not yet made — and finding companies whose future is mispriced.

> Compliance note: this article describes growth investing and its strategy menu analytically, including evidence on what works and what fails. Nothing here is a recommendation.

## Core idea
Where [[value-investing]] hunts for mispriced assets in place, growth investing bets on the future. Damodaran's central — and unsparing — argument is that investing in youth is mostly a *pricing* exercise played by traders, not an intrinsic-value exercise played by investors (see [[pricing-game-vs-value-game]]). Two communities put money to work here: **venture capitalists** in the private domain, and **public-market growth investors** once a company has listed.

VCs do not (and largely cannot) estimate intrinsic value; they *price* a company with no earnings off the last round, off "comparable" private deals, or off public peers, then bet on selling higher to the next buyer (another round, a strategic acquirer, or an IPO). Because that pricing rests on small, infrequently updated, opaque samples, it is noisier, more subjective, more lagged, and more prone to feedback-driven price spirals than public-market pricing. VC payoffs are brutally skewed — a tiny tail of winners returns many multiples while most return little — yet the best VCs win *persistently*, evidence of a durable edge (less-wrong relative pricing, more influence over investees, better timing of entry and especially exit, plus skill at judging narrative, founders and failure risk, and at spreading bets).

## The public growth-investing menu
Once a company lists, growth investors use proxies and screens. The chapter lays out the menu and finds most strands fragile, cyclical, or short-horizon:

- **IPO investing** — buy at the offering for the post-offering pop.
- **Highest expected growth** — screen for the fastest forecast growers.
- **Growth at a reasonable price (GARP)** — buy growth, but only when priced reasonably (the Peter Lynch school).
- **Buying high-PE "growth" names** and **earnings-momentum / forecast-revision trading.**

## Across the life cycle
Growth investing is the **young/high-growth-stage** counterpart to value investing's mature focus — the two sit at opposite ends of the value-vs-growth split (see [[measuring-lifecycle-stage]]). It is the investing-side complement to the *valuation* of the same firms in [[valuing-young-and-startups]] and [[valuing-high-growth]]. Because young firms are trader-dominated, a much larger share of their trading reflects mood and momentum, so prices can be pushed to extremes — which is exactly why a long horizon matters most here. Growth-screen efficacy is also cyclical, tied to the earnings cycle and the yield curve, so market-timing skill can augment returns.

## Mechanics / formulas
- **PEG ratio** (Peter Lynch's signature screen) = PE ÷ expected earnings growth (%); < 1.0 attractive, ≤ 0.5 ideal. **Yield-adjusted PEG** = PE ÷ (EPS growth + dividend yield) for income-paying slower growers.
- **Lynch's six categories** — fast growers (the "ten-bagger" hunting ground), stalwarts, slow growers, cyclicals, turnarounds, and asset plays — each with its own expectations and exit discipline; "invest in what you know."
- **Price per user / EV per subscriber** — the dominant *pricing* metric for pre-revenue firms (price ÷ users/subscribers/downloads). Crude: it treats all users as equal and ignores user growth, churn, acquisition cost and revenue model. See [[value-per-user]] for the proper, disaggregated treatment.
- **IPO allotment / winner's-curse rule** — in underpriced (oversubscribed) deals you get a small allotment; in overpriced deals you are filled — so naïve IPO subscription is structurally adverse-selected.
- **Earnings-revision refinements** — bolder, earlier, and big-broker revisions move prices more, but the effect is short-horizon (weeks–months) and small after costs.

## Pitfalls & nuances
- **IPO underpricing exists; capturing it does not.** IPOs are underpriced on average at the offer, but the allotment problem leaves naïve subscribers underweight the winners. Post-IPO long-run returns are weak — roughly −5.3% vs. the market and about −1.5% style-adjusted over three years.
- **"Hot-market only" IPO buying is a momentum strategy** — it depends on timing the regime (issues at the very end of a hot window are likeliest to fail) and is undiversified (hot-market IPOs cluster by sector).
- **Past growth is a weak signal.** Period-to-period earnings-growth persistence is low, especially for large caps — value depends on *future* growth.
- **Paying up for growth has, on average, destroyed return.** High-PE (top-decile) portfolios historically underperform low-PE ones, more so value-weighted. Buying high expected growth works only if analysts forecast long-term growth well *and* the price does not already reflect it — the evidence works against both.
- **Earnings-momentum / revision edges are fragile** — they lean on the two weakest links (firm earnings reports and analyst forecasts), are short-horizon, and may not survive transaction costs.
- **What actually separates winners:** better growth estimates than the market, a long time horizon (more critical here than anywhere), and macro/market-timing skill — a rare combination.
