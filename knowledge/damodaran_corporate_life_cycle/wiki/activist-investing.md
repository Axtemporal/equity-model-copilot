# Activist Investing

**What it is.** Activist investing is taking a large stake in an undervalued or poorly run firm and using that position to *force* the operating, financing, cash-return or control changes that release the trapped value — becoming the catalyst rather than waiting for one.

> Compliance note: this article describes activism, its targets, levers and success rates analytically. Nothing here is a recommendation.

## Core idea
Passive contrarianism has a fatal flaw: you do not control your destiny — you can buy a badly run firm betting on change that never comes and watch the investment wilt. The activist instead buys *and then changes how the company is run*. This makes activism the headline answer to "how do you actually unlock value in a mature or declining firm?" and the bridge between mature-firm *valuation* and mature-firm *management*. Its levers map directly onto the corporate-finance decisions:

- **Investment / asset side** — shut down, divest, or spin off value-destroying assets and business lines; break up sprawling conglomerates.
- **Financing side** — push an under-levered firm to recapitalize (borrow and buy back stock or pay a dividend) to capture the debt tax shield and impose discipline.
- **Cash-return side** — demand higher dividends or buybacks from firms hoarding cash.
- **Control side** — push the firm to become an acquisition target where it is worth more to someone else; in the extreme, replace management.

Activism is the operational expression of the [[value-of-control]]: the value gap is the difference between the firm's status-quo value and its value under optimal management, and the activist raises the probability that the change actually happens.

## Across the life cycle
The passive-vs-activist axis is U-shaped (see [[investment-philosophies]]). Venture capitalists are almost never passive at the **very young** end, shaping the firms they fund. Activism then **fades through maturity** — the shareholder base turns institutional and passive, and dissatisfied institutions tend to "vote with their feet" (sell) rather than fight. It **revives in decline**, where there is slack to exploit and a wide price-to-value gap. Activists and (non-VC) private equity therefore gravitate to **mature and declining** targets, even though no rule bars them from any stage; in old age, activism shades into the control-and-leverage mechanism of [[private-equity-and-lbo]] and the end-game tactics of liquidators and break-up specialists.

## Mechanics / formulas
- **The investment-decision matrix (business × management × pricing).** Cross three binary judgments — is the *business* good? are the *managers* good? is the *pricing* good (price < value)? A good, cheap business run by *bad* managers is a "Buy & hope for management change" — precisely the target where an activist can manufacture the catalyst.
- **Activist types** — individual activists (Icahn/Peltz-style), institutional/mutual-fund activists, and activist hedge funds — concentrated in Industrials, Consumer Cyclical, Technology and Financials, and in large-cap (>$10bn) and small-cap ($250m–$2bn) bands.
- **Motives, ranked by frequency** — undervaluation first, then capital-structure change, business strategy, sale of the target, and governance change.
- **The attrition funnel** — about two-thirds of activists quit before any formal demand; of those who persist, fewer than 20% seek a board seat, ~10% threaten a proxy fight, and ~7% follow through. Overall, activists get their demands met about **29% of the time**.
- **Success rates by demand** (illustrative): take-private ~41% (highest), restructure operations ~35%, sale to a third party ~32% (most common demand), more dividends/buybacks ~17% (low), cut executive pay ~15% (lowest).

## What activism actually changes
By the chapter's read of the evidence:
- **Operating performance** improves for *individual*-activist targets (ROIC and profitability rise vs. peers) but not clearly for *hedge-fund* targets.
- **Capital structure** rises modestly (~10% higher debt ratios at hedge-fund targets), with dramatic re-levering only in a small subset.
- **Dividend policy** — targets return more cash; payout as a share of earnings rises ~10–20%.
- **Corporate governance** is the *biggest* effect — CEO-turnover likelihood jumps (~5.5% above the prior year), and CEO pay falls and is tied more tightly to performance.

## Pitfalls & nuances
- **Returns are wide-dispersion, cost-laden, and benchmark-sensitive.** Activist mutual funds earn essentially nothing; activist hedge funds earn roughly +7–8% to 20%+ annualized; individuals land in between. But these averages hide high volatility, depend heavily on the chosen benchmark, and shrink toward zero once the large fixed costs of running a campaign (≈$10.7m average) are netted out — the median campaign may merely break even.
- **PE/activists are "no different from any other group of investors"** — fitting neither the hero nor the villain stereotype. The job-destruction and excessive-debt critiques are not well supported by the aggregate data.
- **Activism is the cure for poor management, not for a bad business.** A bad business run by good managers offers no activist upside.
