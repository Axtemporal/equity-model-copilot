# Economic Moat

**What it is.** An economic moat is a firm's durable competitive advantage, and its width and durability are what set the length of the mature plateau — how long margins and returns on capital stay above the cost of capital before fading toward it.

## Core idea

In the [[lifecycle-determinants|geometry of the life-cycle curve]], the moat is the determinant of **flatness**: the wider and more durable the moat, the flatter and longer the plateau a firm can hold at its peak before [[corporate-lifecycle|decline]] sets in. A no-moat, commodity firm has a short, peaked top; a wide-moat firm harvests excess returns for years.

Damodaran organises moats into five types, each available in three widths (the classic Morningstar-style grid, with one exemplar per cell):

| Moat type | Wide | Narrow | No moat |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Brand name** | Coca-Cola (sugar water that commands a price premium) | Snapple (a real brand, weaker pricing power) | Cott (generic, no loyalty) |
| **Switching costs** | Oracle (integrated-database lock-in) | Salesforce (popular but more switchable) | TIBCO (low switching cost) |
| **Network effect** | CME (clearing-house captive volume) | NYSE Euronext (leadership without much network) | Knight Capital (pure order-taker) |
| **Cost advantages** | UPS (logistics scale → low marginal cost) | FedEx (air-express fixed costs, smaller edge) | Con-way (fragmented trucking) |
| **Efficient scale** | International Speedway (the one NASCAR track per metro) | Southern Company (regulated geographic monopoly) | Valero (commodity-refining price-taker) |

## Across the life cycle

The moat's *role* changes with age:

- **Young / high growth:** the moat is a *thesis* — network effects ([[lifecycle-determinants|height driver]]) can compound early dominance into winner-take-all scale, but the advantage is unproven and, in tech, often fleeting ([[compressed-lifecycle-tech]]).
- **Mature growth → mature stable:** the moat is the firm's whole story. The defining challenge of maturity is the **mindset shift from offense to defense** — from gaining share to protecting existing share and margins — and defensibility *is* the moat. Some mature firms see excess returns competed away to zero; others defend the moat and keep earning them.
- **Decline:** the moat has eroded — typically because **disruptors** (young, low-capital, technology-driven entrants) route around conventional moats, or because the advantage was time-limited.

The moat therefore drives both the *plateau* (how long maturity lasts) and the *fade* (how quickly returns converge to the cost of capital in [[terminal-value|terminal value]]).

## Mechanics / formulas

The moat is not a number but a setting that controls the **fade period** in a valuation:

- **Wide, durable moat** → hold margins and ROIC above the cost of capital for many years before fading them; a flatter, longer plateau.
- **Narrow / no moat** → fade fast toward the cost of capital; a short plateau.
- **Encode it as a sourced assumption.** Make moat assessment an explicit cell that sets the fade horizon, rather than burying it in a growth or margin number.
- **Value is created only at a positive spread.** A moat is economically meaningful only insofar as it sustains ROIC above the cost of capital — see [[roic-and-excess-returns]]; growth without that spread does not create value.
- **Disruption check:** if the market price drifts *down* (the price-to-intrinsic gap widens rather than closing), you may be under-weighting moat erosion — raise the probability of failure or haircut margins ([[valuing-mature]]).

## Pitfalls & nuances

- **Moats are not disruption-proof.** Brand, licensing and scale advantages don't immunise a firm; when disruption hits a moated incumbent it is "abrupt and damaging," so even strong firms need contingency plans.
- **Don't lock a moat into terminal value forever.** Freezing today's high margins/ROIC into perpetuity assumes the moat never erodes — appropriate only for genuinely wide, durable moats, and dangerous under disruption risk.
- **Three traits flag a moated incumbent as a disruption target:** a sizeable economic footprint, inefficient production/delivery, and outdated barriers/inertia kept alive by habit and regulation.
- **Mean reversion is a moat assumption in disguise.** Assuming margins revert to historical highs implicitly assumes the moat holds; if it has narrowed, the decline may be permanent ([[measuring-lifecycle-stage]]).
