# Ch 12 — Valuing Mature Businesses

## 1. Core thesis

A mature business derives most of its value from assets **already in place**, not
from future bets, so revenue growth has converged toward the economy's growth
rate, margins are settled, and reinvestment needs have fallen — which means the
firm throws off more cash than it can productively redeploy. Because the "growth
story" is largely written, intrinsic value becomes far less sensitive to growth
assumptions and far **more sensitive to three policy choices the firm controls**:
how it finances itself (debt/equity mix), how it returns cash (dividends and
buybacks), and how well its existing assets are *managed*. That last point is the
chapter's pivot: a mature firm run sub-optimally has a **value of control** — the
gap between its value as currently run and its value if restructured (better
operations, better financing, better cash return). Damodaran extends the
single-firm DCF machinery from [[cap_09_valuation_101]] to the hard cases that
cluster in maturity — multi-business and multinational conglomerates ("the
octopus"), valued by **sum-of-the-parts**; the disruption risk that quietly
erodes incumbents; and the **key-person** premium embedded in some firms. The
running case is Unilever (status-quo value €24.20/share vs. restructured
€36.72/share, a ~one-third uplift driven by activist Nelson Peltz). This sits in
the middle of the life-cycle arc: after young ([[cap_10_valuing_young]]) and
high-growth ([[cap_11_valuing_high_growth]]) firms, before decline
([[cap_13_valuing_declining]]). See [[00_framework_lifecycle]].

> source: Ch12.pdf p.1–11, p.24–26 (slide deck, primary)

## 2. Key concepts & frameworks

### 2.1 What makes a firm "mature" (the financial signature)
Maturity is a bundle of observable traits, not an age. **Revenue growth** is
hard to push much above the economy's growth rate (efficiency gains can still
lift *earnings* growth, but not revenue). **Margins are established** and stable
across cycles. **Competitive advantage** varies — some mature firms see excess
returns compete away to zero or negative, others defend moats and keep earning
excess returns. **Debt capacity rises** as cash flows stabilize (though firms
differ widely in whether they actually use it). **Cash builds up**: rising
earnings plus falling reinvestment needs generate surplus cash that must be
returned or it accumulates. And many mature firms turn to **inorganic growth** —
buying growth via acquisitions — because internal projects no longer move the
needle. Applies to: mature growth and mature stable stages.
> source: Ch12.pdf p.2

### 2.2 The earnings-quality traps of mature firms
Two reasons reported earnings can mislead at mature firms. First, **managed
earnings**: long, stable histories give management both the incentive and the
accounting discretion to smooth/manage reported numbers. Second, **management
inefficiency baked into history**: past earnings reflect how the firm *was* run;
if managers made poor investment or financing choices, reported earnings sit
below what optimal management could deliver. The valuation consequence is sharp —
if a management change is plausible (e.g., an activist on the horizon), then
valuing existing assets off *reported* earnings will **undervalue** them, because
you are implicitly extrapolating sub-optimal management forever. This is the
analytical seed of the value-of-control idea (§2.7).
> source: Ch12.pdf p.3

### 2.3 Why acquisitions become the growth engine (and why that's risky)
As firms mature, internal investment opportunities become scarce relative to
available capital; new projects must be *large* to matter; and businesses with
long lead times between investment and payoff make acquisitions attractive
because they **speed up the payoff** (you buy a built business instead of growing
one). The catch: acquisitions are "an expensive option," and growth bought this
way is only value-creating if the price paid is below the value received. The
modeling implication (§3) is that for a mature firm, acquisitions must be treated
**exactly like capex** — a forecast reinvestment line — not as a free growth
add-on.
> source: Ch12.pdf p.4

### 2.4 Three discount-rate complications unique to mature firms
Mature firms have long price histories and settled risk profiles, so estimating
equity-risk parameters (beta, etc.) from historical data is *more* defensible
than for young/high-growth firms. But three issues distort the cost of capital:
(1) **Complex debt** — debt accumulates from many sources, mixing fixed- and
floating-rate, and multiple currencies; (2) **Mix sensitivity** — costs of debt,
equity, and capital all depend on the debt/equity mix, and a mature firm changing
that mix changes its discount rate; (3) **Acquisition drift** — buying a business
in a different sector or risk class alters the consolidated firm's risk profile
and hence its discount rate. Applies to: all mature firms, acutely the acquisitive
and multinational ones.
> source: Ch12.pdf p.5

### 2.5 Terminal value pitfalls even when growth is "easy"
Because mature growth is already near the economy's rate, terminal value seems
both nearer and simpler than for a growth firm. Two distortions remain.
**Stable growth ≠ stable risk/reinvestment**: to use the terminal-value (Gordon
growth) equation, the firm must also *behave* like a stable firm — average risk
and reinvestment consistent with its stable growth — not just have a low growth
rate. **Locking in inefficiency forever**: if you freeze today's margins, return
on capital, and discount rate into perpetuity and the firm is poorly run, you
undervalue it by assuming bad practice continues eternally — again pointing to
the value-of-control adjustment.
> source: Ch12.pdf p.6

### 2.6 The "Tell a Story" / 3P test for a mature business
Damodaran's valuation discipline (story → numbers → value, from
[[cap_09_valuation_101]]) still applies, but for a mature firm the story is
usually an *extension of history* assembled from **past financials** (past
revenue growth, operating margins over time, amount/type of reinvestment,
earnings variability) plus **market and external information** (sector growth and
profitability, investor base, bond ratings, stock-price volatility) — a
"number-driven valuation story." The **3P test** (is the story Possible? Plausible?
Probable?) seems unnecessary when you are just extrapolating, with two exceptions:
(1) imminent **macroeconomic or regulatory change** that breaks the historical
series, and (2) the business being **disrupted** by new technology or a new
business model. And if your story *departs* from the historical path (even a
merited departure), you must test whether **management can actually be changed** to
deliver it — you cannot assume a turnaround the incumbents won't execute.
> source: Ch12.pdf p.7 (framework diagram, read as image), p.8

### 2.7 The value of control (the chapter's central restructuring framework)
This is the load-bearing concept. The **value of control** quantifies what
better management is worth:

> Value of control = (Probability you can change management) × (Change in firm
> value from changing management)

The first term is a **probability** built from governance frictions that protect
incumbents — takeover restrictions, voting rules and dual-class shares, access to
funds (can a challenger finance a stake?), and the **size** of the company (bigger
= harder to take over). The second term is the **value gap** = value of the firm
run *optimally* minus value of the firm run *as-is*. The key insight: this gap is
**larger at badly managed firms relative to well-managed firms** — a well-run firm
has little control value because there is little to fix. The four restructuring
levers that close the gap map onto the corporate-finance decisions
([[cap_05_corpfin_101]], [[cap_07_financing]], [[cap_08_cash_return]]):

- **Operating / asset management** — run existing assets better (raise margins
  and ROC toward peers; divest assets earning below the cost of capital).
- **Investment / growth policy** — fix under-investment (reinvest more when ROC >
  cost of capital) or over-investment (cut value-destroying reinvestment so ROC ≥
  cost of capital); exploit unused strategic/competitive advantages.
- **Financing policy** — move the debt ratio toward optimal (lever up if too
  conservative, de-lever if over-borrowed), and fix the *type* of financing
  (match debt to assets via swaps/derivatives/refinancing to lower the cost of
  debt and thus the cost of capital).
- **Cash / dividend policy** — return excess cash the market distrusts in the
  firm's hands (dividends/buybacks); be transparent about, or divest,
  value-destroying cross-holdings.

> source: Ch12.pdf p.22 (framework diagram, read as image), p.23 (restructuring
> table, read as image)

### 2.8 The activist-investor trigger
Valuing a mature firm off its existing statements, financing mix, and dividend
policy means **accepting incumbent management as a given**. When an **activist**
(an investor with enough capital to take a meaningful stake and push for change)
appears, that is a signal to **re-value** the firm — because the probability term
in the value-of-control equation just jumped. The chapter ties this to Unilever:
shareholder restlessness, a failed GSK bid in 2022, and Nelson Peltz acquiring a
~1.5% stake and a board seat, arguing the firm has too many brands across too
many geographies and businesses. Whether the activist *wins* is almost beside the
point — the value comes from **making management accountable**.
> source: Ch12.pdf p.11, p.24

### 2.9 Disruption of mature incumbents
A market-price-below-intrinsic-value gap that *widens* (price drifts down, not up
toward your estimate) is a warning that you may be under-weighting **disruption**.
Three traits make a mature business a disruption target: (1) a **sizeable
economic footprint** — the more money spent in a business, the more attractive it
is to disrupt; (2) **inefficient production/delivery** where neither producers nor
consumers are happy; (3) **outdated barriers and inertia** — incumbents survive on
consumer habit plus regulatory/licensing protections and capital/knowledge/
technology entry barriers that have outlived their purpose. The valuation
response (a decision flowchart in the deck) asks: what is the value added by
*growth* assets, how risky are cash flows from *both* existing and growth assets,
how does a disruptor change the operating-risk characteristics and possibly raise
the **probability of failure**, and *when* will the firm itself become mature?
> source: Ch12.pdf p.20 (disruption traits), p.21 (response flowchart, read as image)

### 2.10 "The octopus" — multi-business, multinational firms (SOTP)
Maturity is where firms sprawl across businesses and countries, becoming
"octopuses" whose tentacles each have different risk, growth, and cash-flow
profiles. Two valuation routes (developed fully in §6.3): **consolidated**
(weighted-average risk parameters discounting consolidated cash flows — simple but
the weights drift as segments grow at different rates) versus **sum-of-the-parts
(SOTP)** (value each business/region separately with its own growth, margin, and
discount rate, then add). Damodaran prefers SOTP for genuinely heterogeneous
firms because consolidated averages silently misprice the mix. Relative valuation
struggles here — no single firm is "comparable" to a unique conglomerate, so you
either widen the peer set and control for fundamentals, or run **SOTP relative
valuation** (pick the best multiple per business from a sector regression).
> source: Ch12.pdf p.12–13, p.16–17 (and reading §6.3)

### 2.11 Key-person value
Some firms' value depends disproportionately on one (or few) individuals — a
star founder, scientist, or CEO. Key-person value = value of the firm *with* the
key person − value *without* (estimated by re-running the DCF under the person's
absence: lower growth, lower margins, higher risk, or higher failure
probability). It is **largest at small, young, narrow firms and smallest at large,
mature, diversified ones**, where organizational scale and entrenched competitive
advantages dilute any single person's impact — a tidy life-cycle gradient
(developed in §6.1).
> source: Ch12.pdf p.2–3 (management-dependence framing); blog §6.1

## 3. Metrics, formulas & rules of thumb

**Value of control (master equation).**
`Value of control = P(can change management) × (Value if optimally run − Value as currently run)`
The first factor falls with takeover restrictions, concentrated/super-voting
ownership, financing barriers, and company size; the second is the restructuring
upside, larger the worse the incumbent management. Activist entry raises the first
factor → re-value.

**Restructured vs. status-quo DCF (the operational form of value of control).**
Run the *same* FCFF DCF twice with two assumption sets and compare value/share.
Unilever's status-quo vs. restructured levers (Ch12.pdf p.25):

| Input | Status quo | Restructured | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue growth | 2% for years 1–10 | 3% years 1–5, 2% years 6–10 | Higher growth in India & China |
| Pre-tax operating margin | 18% | 20% | More focus on higher-margin personal care |
| Sales-to-capital ratio | 1.80 | 2.50 | Fewer (value-destroying) acquisitions |
| Cost of capital | 8.97% → 8.97% | 8.00% → 8.00% | Optimizing the financing mix |

Result: value/share rises from **€24.20 (status quo) to €36.72 (restructured)**,
~+33%.

**Sales-to-capital ratio (reinvestment driver).** `Reinvestment = ΔRevenue ÷
Sales-to-capital`. Higher sales-to-capital = more capital-efficient growth =
less reinvestment per unit of revenue = higher FCFF/value. In the Unilever model,
restructuring lifts it from 1.80 to 2.50 by cutting wasteful acquisitions. (See
[[cap_06_investing]] / [[cap_05_corpfin_101]] for ROC vs. cost of
capital as the value-creation test.)

**Reinvestment via acquisitions = capex.** Forecast average annual acquisition
spend and run it through the reinvestment line; value is created only if the
implied ROC on that spend exceeds the cost of capital.

**Terminal value sanity checks.** Stable growth ≤ economy growth (risk-free rate
is a practical ceiling); risk parameters must be *average/stable*, not frozen
high; do **not** lock in current sub-optimal margins/ROC into perpetuity if a fix
is plausible. (TV mechanics per [[cap_09_valuation_101]].)

**Pricing multiples for mature firms — the EV/EBITDA emphasis.** Mature firms
have a "luxury of choices" for the scaling variable (revenues, earnings, book
value). The chapter's dataset is **EV/EBITDA** (`vebitda.xls`). Enterprise-value
multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT, EV/Revenues, EV/Capital) are preferred for
mature/multi-business firms because (a) they are capital-structure-neutral, useful
when mature firms carry heavy and varied debt, and (b) EBITDA/operating income
exist at the *divisional* level for SOTP even when net income/EPS do not.

**Pricing discipline (de-biasing).** *Fight bias* — choose the multiple **before**
seeing each one's answer, guided by sector convention and business sense. *Go
global* — clean accounting differences across countries and measure/embed country
risk in the discount rate. *Control for moats and disruption exposure* when
comparing peers. Expect higher margins, higher ROC, and higher growth → higher
multiples.

**Unilever PE regression (controlling for growth, Ch12.pdf p.19).**
`PE = 19.30 + 152.65 × (Growth rate)`, R² = 37.94%. Predicted PE for Unilever at
5.03% growth = `19.30 + 152.65 × 0.0503 ≈ 26.98`. Trading at **19.13×**, Unilever
screens **underpriced** even after adjusting for its low growth.

**SOTP value (octopus).** `Firm value = Σ value(business_i, region_i)` each with
its own growth, margin, discount rate; then subtract **capitalized centralized
corporate costs**, add cash, subtract debt and minority interests (at *fair*, not
book, value), value/add cross-holdings, and adjust for options to reach equity
value/share.

**Key-person value.** `= Value(firm with key person) − Value(firm without)`;
alternatives are **replacement cost** (works for measurable-skill roles) and
**key-person insurance premiums** (a monetized proxy).

> source: Ch12.pdf p.2–6, p.16–19, p.22–26; readings §6.1, §6.3

## 4. Examples & cases

- **Unilever (running case).** Mature consumer-staples octopus across Beauty &
  Personal Care (most profitable, ~21.66% operating margin in 2016), Home Care
  (laggard), and Foods & Refreshment (shrinking 2016–2021). Large acquisitions —
  **Bestfoods $24.3B (2000), Horlicks $3.8B (2018), Dollar Shave Club $1.0B
  (2016)** — that "clearly have not contributed to revenue growth in a noticeable
  way," illustrating value-destroying inorganic growth (low sales-to-capital).
  Status-quo DCF: €24.20/share at 8.97% cost of capital, 2% growth, 18% margin.
  Activist **Nelson Peltz** (~1.5% stake + board seat after the failed 2022 GSK
  bid) crystallizes the restructuring thesis → restructured value €36.72/share
  (+~33%). PE-vs-growth regression flags it underpriced. The whole Unilever thread
  is the chapter's demonstration of value-of-control valuation.
  > source: Ch12.pdf p.12–15, p.19, p.24–26
- **United Technologies (octopus SOTP, from the reading).** Six divisions
  (Carrier, Pratt & Whitney, Otis, UTC Fire & Security, Hamilton Sundstrand,
  Sikorsky) valued separately by DCF *and* by relative valuation — each business
  assigned the multiple with the highest sector-regression R² (e.g., EV/EBITDA for
  refrigeration & construction, EV/Revenues for defense & industrial products,
  EV/Capital for security & aircraft). SOTP relative value of operating divisions
  ≈ $74.2B, then ~$3.8B of capitalized corporate expense subtracted → ~$70.6B,
  close to the intrinsic estimates.
  > source: octopus.pdf, Illustrations 1–3
- **Coca-Cola (averaging/cross-holding cautionary tale).** Rewarded with a growth
  premium for emerging-market expansion in the 1980s–90s with no offsetting
  country-risk adjustment — exposed only by Russia's late-1990s turmoil; and its
  sub-50% bottling stake (spun off in the 1980s to escape consolidation) must be
  separately valued and partly attributed to the parent. Illustrates both the
  "averaging argument" error and cross-holding valuation.
  > source: octopus.pdf
- **GE / Siemens (the archetypal octopuses).** Used to show that the *consolidated*
  beta is useless for valuing a single division (GE's company beta says nothing
  about its aircraft business) and that no comparable firm exists for a unique
  conglomerate.
  > source: octopus.pdf
- **Pharma vs. biotech (life-cycle divergence, blog §6.2).** Mature pharma (flat
  revenues, ~15–16% margins, collapsing growth-to-R&D ratio) vs. younger biotech
  (the actual growth engine) — a real-sector illustration that maturity reorders
  priorities toward consolidation, capital structure, and tax management rather
  than growth.
  > source: blog §6.2

## 6. Supplementary readings — distilled

### 6.1 Blog — "The Difference Makers: Key Person(s) Valuation" (Damodaran, Dec 2023)
*Read in full via WebFetch.* Damodaran formalizes valuing a key individual as the
difference between the firm's value **with** and **without** that person — re-run
the DCF assuming the person's departure forces lower cash flows, lower growth,
higher risk, or a higher probability of failure, and the value drop is the
key-person value. Two alternative routes: **replacement cost** (estimate the spend
to hire a comparable substitute — workable for roles with specific, measurable
skills like scientists/engineers, weaker for diffuse-influence CEOs) and
**key-person insurance** (premiums monetize the loss). The life-cycle punchline,
which is exactly why this maps to a *mature*-firm chapter: key-person value
**shrinks as firms grow and mature** — a star trader matters enormously at a
10-person shop but is nearly negligible at a large bank; entrenched competitive
advantages and organizational scale reduce dependence on any one person. Beyond
the slides, it supplies the explicit three-method toolkit and the size/maturity
gradient. *Read in full.*
> source: https://aswathdamodaran.blogspot.com/2023/12/the-difference-makers-key-persons.html

### 6.2 Blog — "Divergence in the Drug Businesses: Pharmaceuticals and Biotechnology" (Damodaran, Nov 2015)
*Read in full via WebFetch.* A sector case study of maturity through a life-cycle
lens: legacy **pharma** has gone flat (roughly flat revenues 2010–2014, operating
margins ~15–16%) while **biotech** became the industry's actual growth engine
(biotech's revenue share rose 19.2%→30.6% and market-cap share 26.9%→38.2% over
2010–2014). The diagnostic metric is a **"growth-to-R&D ratio"** that collapsed to
~0.08 for pharma (almost no revenue growth per R&D dollar) vs. ~0.95 for biotech —
an R&D-productivity crisis that *is* the signature of maturation. Implication for
valuing mature pharma: stop modeling it as a growth firm; expect **less aggregate
R&D, and priorities shifting to consolidation, capital-structure changes, and tax
management** — precisely the financing/cash-return/control levers this chapter
emphasizes. Beyond the slides, it shows how to *empirically* spot maturity inside
a sector and warns against over-crediting a mature incumbent for growth it no
longer generates. *Read in full.*
> source: https://aswathdamodaran.blogspot.com/2015/11/divergence-in-drug-businesses.html

### 6.3 Reading — "The Octopus: Valuing Multi-business, Multi-national Companies" (Damodaran, SSRN 1609795, Nov 2009)
*Read in full via the public Stern PDF (`pdfiles/papers/octopus.pdf`); the SSRN
abstract page itself returned HTTP 403, so the full-text PDF was used.* The paper
is the deep treatment behind §2.10. Multi-business, multinational firms are
"bundled and sold as a single package," and six shared traits create estimation
headaches: many countries/markets (cash flows of differing risk needing differing
discount rates), risk parameters available only for the aggregate not the pieces,
tax rates that blend jurisdictions, large **centralized costs** allocated across
divisions by arbitrary keys (corrupting divisional earnings), **intra-company
transactions** (inflating gross revenues/costs and shifting profits between
divisions at non-market transfer prices), and **complex holding structures**
(majority/minority cross-holdings). It contrasts two DCF approaches:
**consolidated** (weighted-average risk parameters on consolidated cash flows —
technically valid but the weights drift as segments grow at different rates, so
inputs must be re-weighted forward) versus **sum-of-the-parts** (value each
business/region with its own growth, margin, and discount rate — risky/emerging
streams discounted harder than safe/developed ones — then add). Two recurring
errors ("the dark side"): for *multinationals*, judging risk by **country of
incorporation** rather than **operations** (so a developed-market HQ escapes
country-risk adjustment while claiming emerging-market growth → systematic
overvaluation, the Coca-Cola pattern); and for *multi-business*, the "**averaging
argument**" — using consolidated margins/ROC/beta as if the current mix weights
hold forever. On **cross-holdings**: minority stakes are often (lazily) carried at
book; majority stakes are fully consolidated with the outside share booked as
minority interest — both should be re-stated to **fair/market value**, and majority
holdings valued separately and added back. **Relative valuation** can't find a
true comparable for a conglomerate, so either widen the peer set and control for
fundamentals, or do **SOTP relative valuation** — illustrated on **United
Technologies**: pick, per division, the EV multiple with the highest
sector-regression R² (EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenues, EV/EBIT, or EV/Capital), value each
division, sum (~$74.2B), then subtract capitalized corporate overhead (~$3.8B) →
~$70.6B, before the usual cash/debt/minority/options bridge to equity. Whether to
add a **synergy premium** or a **conglomerate discount** (lost focus / governance
drag) is a judgment on top. Adds well beyond the slides: the full SOTP mechanics,
the incorporation-vs-operations country-risk rule, centralized-cost/intra-company
adjustments, the cross-holding fair-value rule, and worked DCF + relative
examples. *Read in full.*
> source: https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/pdfiles/papers/octopus.pdf (SSRN
> abstract https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1609795 — 403)

## 7. Takeaways for valuation & modeling

For an analyst building standardized models (raw material for
[[application_to_copilot]]):

1. **For a mature firm, spend assumption effort on financing and cash return, not
   growth.** Value is dominated by assets-in-place and is most sensitive to the
   debt/equity mix and payout policy. The copilot should down-weight growth
   debates and interrogate the **debt schedule** (mix, type, cost — feeding WACC by
   formula per the build backlog) and the **payout/buyback** lines hardest for
   mature pilots.

2. **Build a status-quo vs. restructured (value-of-control) view into the
   Valuation tab.** Run the same FCFF DCF twice — once on incumbent policy, once
   on optimized operating margin, sales-to-capital, and cost of capital — and
   report the gap as the value of control (label it analytically, never as a
   recommendation, per compliance). This aligns directly with the planned
   bull/base/bear scenario toggle: the restructured case is a structured,
   justified deviation from base. Trigger a re-valuation when an activist appears.

3. **Treat acquisitions as reinvestment (capex), and police it with ROC vs. cost
   of capital.** For acquisitive mature firms, forecast average acquisition spend
   on the reinvestment line and flag value destruction whenever implied ROC < cost
   of capital (the Unilever sales-to-capital lesson). The sales-to-capital ratio
   is the single most useful efficiency-of-growth driver to expose as an assumption
   cell.

4. **Use EV/EBITDA (and EV-family) multiples for mature-firm pricing, chosen
   before seeing the answer.** EV multiples are capital-structure-neutral (vital
   for heavily/variously levered mature firms) and survive to the divisional level
   for SOTP. Pull industry averages from `vebitda.xls`; pre-commit to the multiple
   by sector convention to fight bias; control comps for growth, margin, ROC, moat,
   and disruption exposure.

5. **Value octopuses by sum-of-the-parts, with per-segment discount rates and a
   fair-value bridge.** For multi-business/multinational pilots (a real risk in
   Brazilian conglomerates), value each segment/region with its own growth, margin,
   and cost of capital; embed **country risk by operations, not incorporation**;
   correct divisional earnings for centralized-cost allocation and intra-company
   transfer pricing; and restate cross-holdings and minority interest at **fair
   value, not book**, in the EV→equity bridge. Keep one currency consistent across
   cash flows and discount rate.

6. **Don't lock sub-optimal practice into terminal value, and stress-test for
   disruption.** In the TV, use *average/stable* risk and reinvestment and avoid
   freezing today's poor margins/ROC forever. Add a disruption check: if the
   intrinsic-vs-price gap widens rather than closes, raise the probability of
   failure or haircut margins — and consider a key-person sensitivity for
   founder-dependent firms (largest impact at smaller, narrower firms).
