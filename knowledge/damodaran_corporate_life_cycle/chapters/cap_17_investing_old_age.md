# Ch 17 — Investing in Old Age (Shrinking and Declining Businesses)

## 1. Core thesis

This chapter completes the investing block by turning to the last act of the
life cycle: how investors can make money in **mature, shrinking and declining**
companies — the firms most other philosophies avoid. Damodaran's organizing
claim is that the *mechanism* for old-age investing is fundamentally about
**control and restructuring**, not patient buy-and-hold. The two archetypal
players are **private equity (in its non-VC, buyout form)** and **activist
investors**, and the chapter argues that although neither is logically barred
from any stage, the way they create value — fixing operations, re-levering,
forcing cash return, breaking up or liquidating — naturally pulls them toward
mature and declining targets where there is slack to exploit and a gap between
price and value to close. Below the activists sit the **vulture / distressed
investors** (liquidators, break-up specialists, and traders) who profit from the
end-game itself: bankruptcy, liquidation, reorganization and the mispricing that
swirls around distress. The throughline is that **value in old age comes from
changing the firm or its claim structure**, not from waiting for organic growth.
This sits directly downstream of [[cap_13_valuing_declining]] (how to
value these firms) and [[cap_16_investing_middle_age]] (value investing in
mature firms), and feeds the management response in
[[cap_19_fighting_aging]]. It is the investing-side counterpart of the
"act your age" discipline in [[00_framework_lifecycle]].

> source: Ch17.pdf p.1–2, p.18 (slide deck, primary)

## 2. Key concepts & frameworks

### 2.1 Why PE and activists gravitate to mature/declining firms
Damodaran opens by noting there is no rule preventing private equity (non-VC) or
activist investors from targeting any life-cycle stage. But because *how they add
value* is by taking control and changing how a business is run, financed or
structured, their natural prey is the **mature-and-declining** firm, where
incumbent management has left value on the table. The chapter shows the
life-cycle connection visually: a sequence of ownership-mix pie charts running
Start-up → Build-up → IPO → Young-growth-public → Growth-public → Mature-public,
illustrating that buyouts re-concentrate ownership in the later (mature) phases
rather than the venture phases.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.2 (diagram slide; read as image)

### 2.2 Private equity / LBOs as the old-age mechanism
Buyouts are the dominant vehicle. The deck tracks the **growth of US leveraged
buyouts (2000–2021)** — a rising, lumpy series that spikes hardest in the most
recent year — and catalogs the **largest PE investors in 2021** (KKR ~$126.5bn
capital raised, Blackstone ~$82.5bn, EQT, CVC, Thoma Bravo, Carlyle, General
Atlantic, Clearlake, Hellman & Friedman, Insight Partners). The takeaway is
scale: PE is now a structural feature of public markets, not a fringe activity,
and its capital concentrates in a handful of mega-firms.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.3–4 (chart + table slides; read as images)

### 2.3 What makes a "best" buyout / activist target
Three screening characteristics define an attractive target:
- **Large free cash flows** — because buyouts are debt-funded, the target must
  throw off enough operating cash to service and pay down that debt.
- **Cheap** — acquiring below intrinsic value mechanically improves the odds of a
  high exit return, even when the plan is to "fix and flip."
- **Potential for operating improvement** — proxied (imperfectly) by operating
  margins or ROIC lagging the peer group; the evidence supports the idea that
  laggards in profitability/returns are disproportionately targeted.

This is the investing-side restatement of the decline diagnostics in
[[cap_13_valuing_declining]].
> source: Ch17.pdf p.6 (text slide)

### 2.4 Activist investing — who, where, and how often it works
Damodaran separates activists into **individual activists** (e.g. Icahn-style),
**institutional/mutual-fund activists**, and **activist hedge funds**, and shows
they spread across sectors (Industrials, Consumer Cyclical, Technology and
Financials are the heaviest activist sectors 2018–2020) and across market caps
(large-cap >$10bn and small-cap $250m–$2bn are the most-targeted bands). The
**motives behind targeting** rank, by frequency: undervaluation first, then
capital-structure change, business strategy, sale of the target, and governance
change.

The process is a funnel with heavy attrition: about **two-thirds of activists
quit before making any formal demand**; of those who persist, fewer than 20% ask
for a board seat, ~10% threaten a proxy fight, and only ~7% follow through.
Overall, activists get their demands met about **29% of the time**.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.5, p.7–8, p.10–11 (chart/table + text slides; read as images)

### 2.5 What activism actually changes at the target
Post-activism effects, by the chapter's read of the evidence:
- **Operating performance** — improves for firms targeted by *individual*
  activists (ROIC and profitability rise vs. peers) but **not** clearly for
  *hedge-fund* targets.
- **Capital structure** — a modest ~10% increase in debt ratios at hedge-fund
  targets (not dramatic, not statistically robust), with dramatic re-levering
  only in a small subset.
- **Dividend policy** — targets return more cash, with payout as a share of
  earnings rising ~10–20%.
- **Corporate governance** — the **biggest** effect: CEO-turnover likelihood
  jumps (~5.5% above the prior year), and CEO pay falls and is tied more tightly
  to performance.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.9, p.12 (table + text slides; read as images)

### 2.6 The returns to PE and activism (and their fragility)
- **Activist mutual funds** earn essentially nothing — markets shrug at their
  proxy proposals (little/no price reaction).
- **Activist hedge funds** earn substantial annualized excess returns, roughly
  **7–8% at the low end to 20%+ at the high end**.
- **Individual activists** land in between.
- Crucially, these averages hide **high volatility**, are **sensitive to the
  benchmark and risk-adjustment** chosen, and (per the supplementary blog, §6.2)
  shrink toward zero once the large fixed **costs of running a campaign** are
  netted out — the median activist may merely break even.

The deck also shows **excess returns around buyouts** (abnormal share turnover
and abnormal buy-and-hold returns both climbing into the deal) and **PE "alpha"
over time**, reinforcing that the edge is real but uneven.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.13–16 (chart slides; read as images)

### 2.7 Vulture / distressed investing — the three players
For firms past saving, a separate investor class profits from the end-game:
- **The Liquidators** — buy firms whose **liquidation (asset) value exceeds
  their going-concern value** under denial-prone management, acquire them, and
  sell the assets for cash.
- **The Break-up Specialists** — share the liquidators' premise that the firm
  shouldn't continue *whole*, but instead **split / spin off** it into sub-parts
  that each survive as going concerns.
- **The Traders** — believe trading profits exist *during* decline and distress,
  exploiting the **legal mechanics of bankruptcy/restructuring** and trading
  ahead of the market on developments — on either equity or debt.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.18 (text slide)

### 2.8 Voluntary liquidation — process and economics
The liquidation route (outside bankruptcy): the **board must assent**, then a
**liquidator** is appointed who sells assets for cash with legal authority to act
for the firm, and distributes proceeds in the **absolute-priority order** —
senior debt first, then subordinated debt and mezzanine, with any residual to
equity. Keys to making liquidation investing pay: (a) **don't overbid above
liquidation value**; (b) **control the costs of liquidating** (fire-sale
discounts for speed, taxes); (c) it works best when **assets are liquid** and the
**market value sits far below liquidation value**.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.22–23 (text slides)

### 2.9 Break-ups — spin-offs, split-offs, split-ups, and their pros/cons
Three structural forms:
- **Spin-off** — parent carves out a subsidiary/geography into an independent
  company; existing parent shareholders get **proportional** shares in the new
  entity.
- **Split-off** — same carve-out, but parent holders **choose** to swap their
  parent shares for the new company's shares or stay in the parent.
- **Split-up** — the parent is broken into two+ entities and **liquidated**, with
  holders receiving shares in the emerging entities.

**Why break up (benefits):** the market is undervaluing the whole vs. the sum of
parts; a **"contaminated" division** (e.g. litigation-exposed tobacco) is
dragging the rest; an **efficiency story** (managers spread thin /
cross-subsidization); a **simplicity story** (bite-size, easier-to-value pieces,
especially in crisis markets); and a **tax story** (complex codes let a break-up
lower the bill).

**Why not (costs):** **lost economies of scale** (shared advertising/
distribution), **reduced/costlier access to capital** when internal capital
markets had let cash-rich units fund cash-poor ones, and **lost synergies** where
businesses fed off each other. The investing implication: a break-up only creates
value when the sum-of-parts gain outweighs these consolidation benefits.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.24–26 (text slides)

### 2.10 Distress and bankruptcy — the choices and the payoff paths
Under bankruptcy a firm faces three resolutions:
- **Liquidation** — like voluntary liquidation but the liquidator answers to the
  **bankruptcy court** (not the board), and court-driven liquidations tend to be
  **more costly**, eating into proceeds.
- **Reorganization** — a plan voted on by creditors, bondholders and shareholders
  and confirmed by the court (which can override the vote and confirm anyway if
  the plan treats claimants fairly).
- **Merger** — in a subset of cases, merging with a **healthy firm** relieves or
  defers distress and provides an exit.

Distressed investors' **pathways to excess return**: (1) an **operating
comeback** (the cleanest exit — return to profitability); (2) a
**liquidation/merger valuation** windfall (proceeds or a buyer's premium exceed
expectations); (3) the **reorganization sweepstakes**, where the restructuring of
claims makes some claimholders better off at others' expense; and (4) **market
mispricing**, where traders buy underpriced / short overpriced distressed
securities, occasionally locking in near-arbitrage.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.27–28 (text slides)

### 2.11 Empirical texture of vulture investing
The chapter closes with evidence: a rising count of **spin-off deals** over time
and a scatter of the **top spin-offs**; **returns to spin-offs** (the spun-off
entity outperforms the market by a few percent over ~60 days post-separation
while the **parent underperforms**); the **growth of low-rated corporate debt**
(high-yield + leveraged loans) and of **distressed-debt funds**; and the pattern
of **risk-adjusted equity and bond returns around distress** (both decline into
the distress event). Aggregating, **vulture/distressed strategies** show positive
average payoffs (equity, convertible/credit hedge, and distressed hedge funds all
positive on average), again with wide dispersion.
> source: Ch17.pdf p.19–21, p.29–31 (chart slides; read as images)

## 3. Metrics, formulas & rules of thumb

The chapter is more about mechanics and screening than closed-form formulas, but
it yields a dense set of decision rules. **LBO/distress value drivers and
distress-investing logic:**

**LBO value drivers (the "anatomy," cross-referenced from §6.3).** An LBO bundles
three separable bets, each with its own value test:
1. **Leverage** — borrowing a large share of value. Adds value chiefly through
   the **interest tax shield** and the discipline that debt service imposes on
   free cash flow; the target therefore needs **large, stable FCF** to service
   it. Over-levering a cyclical or low-FCF target is the classic failure mode.
2. **Control** — taking an active management role to drive **operating
   improvement** (margin/ROIC catch-up to peers). Value here = the size of the
   operating gap that can realistically be closed.
3. **Going private** — removing public-market frictions/short-termism. A
   genuine-but-smaller source of value, and **separable** from the other two —
   bundling all three as mandatory in every deal is a mistake.

**Screening heuristics for a buyout/activist target:**
- High and stable **free cash flow** (debt-serviceability test).
- **Price below intrinsic value** (cheapness improves exit returns mechanically).
- **Operating margin or ROIC below the peer group** (operating-improvement
  potential).

**Liquidation rules of thumb:**
- Invest only when **liquidation value > going-concern (market) value**.
- **Acquisition price must stay below liquidation value** net of costs.
- Subtract **fire-sale discounts and taxes**; success scales with **asset
  liquidity** and the **size of the value gap**.
- Distribute in **absolute priority**: senior debt → subordinated/mezzanine →
  equity residual.

**Break-up decision rule:** break up only if (sum-of-parts value − consolidation
benefits lost: economies of scale + internal-capital-market access + synergies) >
0; favor it most when a contaminated division, conglomerate discount, or crisis
market is depressing the whole.

**Activist success-rate benchmarks** (success rate next to each demand):
| Demand | Frequency / share of campaigns | Success rate |
|---|---|---|
| Take private | ~10.5% | **41%** (highest) |
| Restructure inefficient operations | ~13.7% | 35% |
| Increase disclosure | ~2.8% | 36% |
| Sale of company to 3rd party | ~31.5% (most common) | 32% |
| Increase debt load | ~4.4% | 32% |
| Back out of announced merger | ~12.5% | 29% |
| Convert/other governance, board | — | ~19% |
| More dividends & buybacks | ~15.5% | **17%** (low) |
| Remove CEO / split CEO-Chair | ~5.4% | 19% |
| Cut executive compensation | ~4.0% | **15%** (lowest) |
| **Overall** | — | **~29%** |

**Activism attrition funnel:** ~⅔ quit before any demand → <20% seek a board seat
→ ~10% threaten a proxy fight → ~7% follow through.

**Return benchmarks:** activist mutual funds ≈ 0 excess return; activist hedge
funds ≈ +7–8% to +20%+ annualized (high variance, benchmark-sensitive, and net of
~$10.7m average campaign cost shrinking toward zero); individual activists in
between; spin-offs ≈ a few % outperformance over ~60 days while parents lag.

> source: Ch17.pdf p.6, p.8–12, p.22–28, p.29; §6.3 (SSRN 1162862)

## 4. Examples & cases

- **GE (supplementary blog, §6.1)** — the chapter's natural real-world anchor: a
  126-year-old industrial conglomerate fallen from a >$500bn peak (2001) to ~$72bn
  (Nov 2018), used to dramatize the strategic choices facing a declining
  firm — **break up (~$103bn potential but fire-sale-discount-prone)** vs.
  **orderly retrenchment (~$77bn)** vs. the value-destroying **"Bataan Death
  March" reincarnation gamble**. Damodaran's prescription: act your age, sell the
  weak/capital businesses, deleverage before paying dividends, and pick a
  "liquidator" CEO over a "visionary." See [[cap_13_valuing_declining]]
  and [[cap_19_fighting_aging]].
- **The Harman LBO (aborted; KKR + Goldman) (SSRN 1162862, §6.3)** — the paper's
  case study of how **choosing the wrong target** (insufficient FCF / fit) sinks
  even elite sponsors, proving the leverage/control/going-private components must
  each be justified separately.
- **Tobacco-company spin-offs** — cited as the textbook "contaminated parts"
  break-up: litigation-exposed tobacco dragging down non-tobacco units, which were
  spun off to unlock the latter's value.
- **Large PE firms** — KKR, Blackstone, EQT, CVC, Thoma Bravo, Carlyle, etc., as
  the scale players of buyout capital.
- **Activist archetypes** — individual activists (Icahn/Peltz-style) vs.
  institutional/mutual-fund activists vs. activist hedge funds (Bain, KKR,
  Blackstone-type), contrasted by their differing effects on targets.

> source: Ch17.pdf p.4, p.18, p.25; §6.1 GE blog; §6.3 SSRN 1162862

## 6. Supplementary readings — distilled

### 6.1 Blog — "The GE End Game: Bataan Death March or Turnaround Play?" (Damodaran, Nov 2018)
*Read in full via WebFetch.* Damodaran works GE as a live case of investing in a
declining firm. He places the 126-year-old conglomerate in the
mature-to-declining phase — peak market cap >$500bn (2001) collapsing to ~$72bn
(Nov 2018), with corporate overhead (~$3.83bn plus ~$4.1bn restructuring charges)
wiping out roughly half of operating profit and only aviation, healthcare and
transportation earning above their cost of capital. He lays out three strategic
options: **break it up** (sum-of-parts could fetch ~$103bn on peer EV/EBITDA, but
fire sales of whole companies rarely realize that because buyers hold back);
**retrench and reshape** (~$77bn — exit GE Capital, sell low-margin power/
renewables, concentrate on aviation/healthcare/transportation); and the
**"Bataan Death March"** of reincarnation — throwing cash at new businesses or big
acquisitions to recapture old glory, which he warns destroys value. He urges new
CEO Larry Culp to be "Larry the Liquidator," not "Steve the Visionary." On
**financial policy for a declining firm**, his prescriptions are textbook for the
chapter: prioritize **debt reduction** (GE Capital ~$51bn obligations against
~$27bn estimated value is the core danger), **defer dividends** until the debt is
down and the capital business is exited, **minimize reinvestment**, and **divest
carefully** (desperation, e.g. the discounted Baker Hughes share sale, undermines
pricing power). His sum-of-parts retrenchment value (~$77–85bn) sits above the
~$72bn market cap — the market may have "over-corrected" — but he stresses it is a
**conditional, value-not-trap** bet: opaque accounting, ~$30bn pension
liabilities, inter-unit cash-flow distortions and the risk of a "falling knife"
make the thesis hinge on management discipline ("every valuation has to have a
story"). **What it adds beyond the slides:** a fully worked declining-firm
investment case showing how the abstract liquidate/retrench/reincarnate choices
translate into a concrete sum-of-parts valuation and a behavioral bet on whether
old-age management will "act its age."
> source: https://aswathdamodaran.blogspot.com/2018/11/the-ge-end-game-bataan-death-march-or.html

### 6.2 Blog — "Private Equity: Hero or Villain?" (Damodaran, Jan 2012)
*Read in full via WebFetch.* Damodaran rejects the Gordon-Gekko caricature and
defines PE/activists objectively by three traits — equity stakes, **active
involvement** (not passive), and the **option to take companies private** —
splitting them into individual activists (Icahn, Peltz), institutional/mutual-fund
activists, and activist hedge funds. He identifies **four value-creation
channels** — asset deployment/performance (improves for individual-activist
targets, less so for hedge-fund targets), **capital structure** (modest ~10% debt
increases, with sharp re-levering only in a subset, and bond prices dropping
3–5%), **dividend policy** (cash returned up 10–20% of earnings), and
**governance** (the biggest effect: CEO turnover +5.5%, lower and more
performance-linked pay). He rebuts the **job-destruction** critique (success
should be judged by stockholder value and customer service, not headcount; a
Bain-targeted photo-album firm's layoffs were caused by digital disruption, not PE
ruthlessness) and the **excessive-debt** critique (aggregate data don't support
"going overboard"). On **targets**: individual/institutional activists chase poorly
managed, below-peer firms, while hedge-fund activists paradoxically behave like
passive value investors — an 888-campaign study (2001–2005) found targets are
small/mid-cap, liquid, low price-to-book, **profitable with solid cash flows**,
and overpay their CEOs; the primary motive is **undervaluation**, not turnaround.
On **returns**: activist mutual funds ≈ nothing; activist hedge funds +7–8% to
20%+ annualized; but net of ~$10.71m average campaign costs the edge shrinks
"toward zero," returns are highly skewed (median ≈ break-even), and a PE
investor's *entry* reliably bumps the stock up ("indisputable"). **What it adds
beyond the slides:** the moral/empirical debate behind the chapter's neutral
charts — PE is "no different from any other group of investors," fitting neither
the hero nor villain stereotype — plus the cost-adjustment that tempers the
headline return numbers.
> source: https://aswathdamodaran.blogspot.com/2012/01/private-equity-hero-or-villain.html

### 6.3 Reading — "The Anatomy of an LBO: Leverage, Control and Value" (Damodaran, SSRN 1162862, Jun 2008)
*Abstract-only — SSRN abstract page and PDF returned HTTP 403; distilled from the
fetched abstract via web search (full PDF not retrievable in this environment).*
The paper decomposes a leveraged buyout into **three separable components**:
**leverage** (borrowing a large share of a public firm's value), **control**
(taking an active management role), and **going private** (delisting). None is new
and each can be individually justified from corporate-finance first principles —
the paper works through the conditions under which each makes sense (leverage when
the firm has the FCF and stability to carry tax-advantaged debt; control when
there is a real operating gap to close; going private when public-market
frictions genuinely hurt). Using the **aborted Harman LBO (KKR and Goldman as
lead players)** as a case study, Damodaran argues that **picking the wrong target
is a recipe for disaster even for elite sponsors**, and concludes that because the
three components are separable, **bundling them as mandatory pieces of every deal
is a mistake** — a deal should include only the components its specific target
justifies. **What it adds beyond the slides:** the rigorous "value test" for each
LBO ingredient and the warning that target selection, not deal-making prowess,
governs outcomes — the analytical backbone behind the chapter's "best targets"
screen (§2.3) and the GE retrench-vs-reincarnate framing (§6.1).
> source: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1162862 (abstract-only; HTTP 403 on full text)

## 7. Takeaways for valuation & modeling

For an equity analyst building standardized models of mature/declining Brazilian
equities (raw material for [[application_to_copilot]]):

1. **Model going-concern AND liquidation value for any declining firm.** Old-age
   investing turns on the gap between the two. The Valuation tab for a declining
   company should carry an explicit **liquidation/asset-value** estimate beside
   the DCF, with absolute-priority waterfall (senior debt → subordinated/mezzanine
   → equity) so the model surfaces when liquidation value exceeds equity value —
   the liquidator's signal. Build on [[cap_13_valuing_declining]].

2. **Add a sum-of-the-parts / break-up scenario.** Where a firm has separable
   divisions (conglomerate, or a "contaminated" unit), model each segment with its
   own margin/ROIC/multiple and net out **consolidation benefits lost** (economies
   of scale, internal-capital-market access, synergies) — so the copilot can flag
   a credible break-up gap rather than asserting one. Apply peer EV/EBITDA per
   segment as a cross-check, but discount whole-company sale proceeds for
   fire-sale realism.

3. **Stress-test debt serviceability and the LBO/operating-improvement lens.**
   For a mature, cash-generative target, the buyout screen (large stable FCF;
   price below value; margin/ROIC below peers) doubles as a quality/upside check.
   The debt schedule should test interest coverage and cash-sweep paydown under a
   re-levering scenario, consistent with the engine's revolver/dynamic-debt
   backlog item — interest on opening balance, no circularity.

4. **Encode "act your age" as the default decline policy.** Per the GE case, a
   declining-firm model's defaults should: minimize reinvestment (capex toward
   maintenance), prioritize **deleveraging before payout**, and treat aggressive
   reinvestment/acquisition assumptions ("reincarnation") as a *flagged, justified
   deviation*, never a silent base case. This aligns the assumption-proposal layer
   with the compliance rule (method + source, never advisory).

5. **Treat activist/PE return numbers as wide-dispersion, cost-laden, and
   benchmark-sensitive — never as a point estimate.** When the copilot reasons
   about restructuring upside, present ranges (e.g. activism success ~29% overall,
   with the demand-by-demand success table) and net of campaign costs, mirroring
   Damodaran's caution that headline excess returns shrink toward zero after
   costs. Keep all such figures analytical, never a recommendation.

6. **Carry a distress/bankruptcy decision branch.** For a firm near distress,
   model the three resolutions (liquidation, reorganization, merger-with-healthy-
   firm) and the claim-restructuring waterfall, so debt and equity holders' payoffs
   are explicit. This is the modeling substrate for "reorganization sweepstakes"
   and distressed-security mispricing analysis.

> source: synthesis of Ch17.pdf p.6, p.18, p.22–28 + §6.1 GE blog + §6.3 SSRN 1162862
