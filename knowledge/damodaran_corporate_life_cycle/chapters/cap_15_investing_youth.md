# Ch 15 — Investing in Youth (Young and Growth Businesses)

## 1. Core thesis

This chapter examines the two communities of investors who put money to work
in the early, high-growth stretch of the corporate life cycle: **venture
capitalists** in the private domain and **public-market growth investors** once
the company has listed. Damodaran's central argument is unsparing — investing in
youth is, for the most part, a **pricing game played by traders rather than an
intrinsic-value game played by investors**. VCs do not (and largely cannot)
estimate intrinsic value; they price the company off the last round, off
"comparable" private deals, or off public peers, then bet on selling higher to
the next buyer. Because that pricing rests on small, infrequently updated,
opaque samples, it is noisier, more subjective, more lagged, and more prone to
feedback-driven price spirals than public-market pricing. The payoffs to the
game are brutally skewed: a tiny minority of VC investments returns multiples of
the capital deployed while most return little or nothing, and the few VCs who
win do so persistently — evidence of a durable edge (better relative pricing,
more influence over investees, and superior timing of entry and, above all,
exit). On the public side, the chapter dissects the menu of growth strategies —
IPO investing, screening on high historical or high expected earnings growth,
buying high-P/E "growth" names, growth-at-a-reasonable-price (GARP), and
earnings-momentum/revision trading — and finds most of them fragile, cyclical,
or short-horizon, working only with rare skill (better growth forecasting, a
long horizon, and macro/market timing). The chapter is the "Investing" block's
treatment of the young end of the life-cycle arc introduced in
[[cap_01_unifying_theory]]; it sits beside [[cap_14_investment_philosophies]]
(the philosophy taxonomy) and is the investing-side counterpart to the
*valuation* chapters [[cap_10_valuing_young]] and [[cap_11_valuing_high_growth]].

> source: Ch15.pdf p.1–2, p.9–11, p.27 (slide deck, primary)

## 2. Key concepts & frameworks

### 2.1 VCs as traders, not investors — the pricing game
Damodaran frames venture capital not as intrinsic valuation but as **pricing**:
the VC's job is to attach a number to a company that has no earnings, often no
meaningful revenue, and no physical assets, and then to exit to a later investor
(another VC round, a strategic acquirer, or the public market via IPO) at a
higher number. Three pricing methods, in ascending order of substance:
1. **Recent pricing of the same company** — anchor on what the most recent prior
   round paid, to judge whether your entry price is "reasonable."
2. **Pricing of "similar" private companies** — find deals in the "same business,"
   scale to revenues or (lacking revenue) to a sector-specific common metric
   (users, subscribers, downloads), and apply that to your target. Every word in
   "similar" and "same business" carries subjective judgment.
3. **Pricing off public companies, with post-value adjustments** — only available
   in the rare case where the private firm already has real operating substance
   (revenues, even earnings) and listed peers exist.

Applies to: start-up and young-growth stages.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.2–3

### 2.2 Why VC pricing is structurally worse than public-market pricing
Three data pathologies compound:
- **Small samples** — private-deal comparables are far fewer than the thousands
  of continuously traded public peers.
- **Infrequent updating** — private transactions are sparse and episodic, so the
  "prices" feeding any comparison are stale.
- **Opaque transactions** — public shares are homogeneous claims and value =
  price × shares; extrapolating from a single VC round (with liquidation
  preferences, participation, ratchets and other terms) to whole-company equity
  value is genuinely hard and often mis-done.

These produce five consequences: (1) **more noise/error** in price estimates;
(2) **more subjectivity** (freedom to pick comparables and to "adjust");
(3) **lag** — VC capital and deal counts ebb and flow with the cycle (more deals
and richer pricing in up markets, the reverse in down markets), so private marks
trail public reality; (4) **feedback loops and price spirals** — because samples
are small, one badly priced deal can trigger many more, amplifying moves in both
directions; (5) **the game demands patience** — unlike public pricing that can
be played in minutes, private pricing requires waiting, more so the younger the
company.

Applies to: start-up, young-growth (private).
> source: Ch15.pdf p.4–5

### 2.3 The VC return distribution — high but skewed, with persistence
The deck's chart slides quantify the payoff structure:
- **VC vs PE vs public-equity returns (US, 2002–2021)** — returns rose across
  the period, with the most recent windows showing the sharpest jumps and VC
  outpacing public equity in the strongest years (chart, p.6).
- **Skewed payoffs** — a large share of capital is invested in deals that end up
  worthless or near-worthless, while the value is concentrated in a small tail of
  winners that return many multiples of cost; the annotation notes the big
  winners can pay back several times the original investment, dwarfing the modest
  returns elsewhere (chart, p.7).
- **Persistence / skill** — the best VCs not only beat the best public-market
  investors but do so **consistently across long periods**, implying a more
  durable competitive advantage than public investors enjoy. The continuity is
  explained by a virtuous cycle: top VCs get **better deal terms** and are
  **invited into more (and better) deals** (p.8).

Applies to: the VC ecosystem across the young end of the life cycle.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.6–8 (chart slides; read as images)

### 2.4 What sets the best VCs apart
Two clusters of skill. First (p.9), the generic edges of any successful pricer,
sharpened for the private setting:
- **Relatively better pricing** — all VC prices are "wrong" (they're estimates),
  but the winners' prices are *less wrong*.
- **More influence over investees** — VCs take active roles (informal advising
  to formal board seats), shaping which metrics the company chases, how to
  improve them, and **how and when to cash out** (IPO vs sale).
- **Better timing** — the whole game is timing; you must time entry well and,
  even more critically, **time the exit**.

Second (p.10), the youth-specific competencies:
- **Good judges of narrative** — assess the founder's story, run it through the
  "3P test" (see [[cap_10_valuing_young]] for the possible/plausible/probable
  story-testing logic), and craft your own narrative to anchor the investment.
- **Good judges of founders/managers** — in a young company you are wholly
  dependent on the founder/team to deliver the story; gauging their **capacity
  and trustworthiness** is the differentiator.
- **Protective of their stake as the firm grows** — defend ownership against
  dilution from new capital providers.
- **Good assessors of failure risk** — most small private firms fail (product
  finds no audience, or poor management); the best VCs find the idea-plus-
  management combination that makes success more likely (never guaranteed).
- **Spread the bets** — failure rates stay high, so diversification is essential,
  and **the earlier the stage (e.g., seed), the more diversification matters**.

Applies to: start-up, young-growth (private).
> source: Ch15.pdf p.9–10

### 2.5 Public growth investing — the menu of strategies
Once a company lists, public growth investors use proxies and screens to find
high-growth stocks they believe are undervalued. The chapter lays out the menu
(p.11) and then critiques each:
- **IPO investing** — buy at the offering to capture the post-offering pop.
- **Highest expected revenue/earnings growth** — screen for the fastest growers.
- **Growth at a reasonable price (GARP)** — buy growth, but only when priced
  reasonably (the Lynch school; see §6.3).
- (Implicitly) **buying high-P/E names** and **earnings-momentum/revision
  trading**, each treated below.

Applies to: young-growth and high-growth (public).
> source: Ch15.pdf p.11

### 2.6 IPO investing — underpricing exists, capturing it does not
There is strong evidence IPOs are, on average, **underpriced at the offer**
(charts on first-day returns, p.12) — but the evidence that investors can
*systematically harvest* that underpricing is much weaker. The core obstacle is
the **allotment problem**: when an offering is underpriced and oversubscribed,
you receive a small allotment; when it is overpriced you get your full request.
So a naïve subscriber ends up **underweight the winners and overweight the
losers** (the "winner's curse"). Two ways to try to beat the allotment game,
neither guaranteed: (1) be the beneficiary of a **biased allotment** from the
underwriter (preferential allocation in hot deals); (2) **bet against the herd**
and short newly public stocks, profiting from the subsequent drift down.
Post-IPO long-run returns are weak: the deck's "post-IPO returns" charts show
IPOs underperforming style/market benchmarks over the years following the
offering — long-term (3-year post-offering) IPO returns average **roughly −5.3%
vs the market and about −1.5% on a style-adjusted basis** (median figures around
−6.7% / −3.6%), i.e., new issues lag both a broad-market and a matched-style
portfolio.

Applies to: young-growth / high-growth at listing.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.12–14 (chart slides; read as images) + p.15

### 2.7 IPO timing — the "hot market" momentum strategy and its risks
IPO activity is intensely cyclical: floods of offerings with heavy underpricing
in hot windows, droughts with little underpricing in cold ones. A strategy of
**only buying IPOs in hot markets** is effectively a **momentum strategy** and
carries momentum's risks: (1) returns hinge on **timing the regime** — entering
late in the hot phase clips returns, and the issues at the very end of a hot
window are the most likely to fail; (2) hot-market IPOs **cluster by sector**, so
a portfolio of "this period's IPOs" is **undiversified**, concentrated in
whatever sector is in vogue.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.16

### 2.8 Selective IPO investing — value the prospectus
Rather than buy every IPO or every hot-market IPO, invest **only where the odds
of underpricing are greatest**, by valuing the company from its prospectus and
filings (intrinsic or relative). Two pitfalls: (1) your **valuation/pricing
skills must be excellent**, because valuing a company *going* public is materially
harder than valuing one already listed; (2) because IPO markets cycle, in cold
markets you may be reduced to finding the **least over-priced** rather than truly
under-priced issues, and you then need an explicit **exit plan** before the
correction.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.17

### 2.9 Growth screens I — historical growth (weak signal)
Screening on **past** earnings growth is a poor strategy. The chart (p.20)
showing the **correlation between earnings growth in successive periods** across
large-, mid-, and small-cap firms makes the point: period-to-period growth
persistence is **low**, especially for large caps, so past growth is a weak
predictor of future growth. Value depends on **future** growth, not past growth.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.19–20 (chart slide read as image)

### 2.10 Growth screens II — expected growth and high-P/E stocks
Since value is driven by *expected* growth, buy stocks with **high analyst-
forecast growth** — but two conditions must hold for excess returns: (1) analysts
must be **good at forecasting long-term growth**, and (2) the market price must
**not already reflect** that growth. The evidence works **against both**.
Relatedly, simply **buying high-P/E "growth" stocks** is shown to underperform:
the deck's chart (p.21) reports that a portfolio of **high-P/E (top decile)**
stocks earns **lower** returns than low-P/E stocks (1927–2023), and that the
effect is **stronger on a value-weighted than an equal-weighted basis** — i.e.,
paying up for growth, on average, has destroyed return.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.18–19, p.21 (chart slide read as image)

### 2.11 Growth cycles — when growth screens work
Growth-screen efficacy is itself **cyclical**. The chart slides (p.22 "earnings
growth" cycles; p.23 "yield curve" cycles) plot the **return difference of growth
vs value strategies** against the **earnings-growth cycle** and against a
**yield-curve / interest-rate signal** (e.g., 10-year minus 3-month spread).
There are extended windows where growth screens work exceptionally well and other
windows where they are counterproductive; some of these swings track the
**earnings cycle** and the **macro/rate environment**, so the ability to **time
these cycles** (a market-timing skill) can substantially augment returns.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.22–23 (chart slides read as images), p.27

### 2.12 Earnings momentum and forecast-revision strategies
A distinct family of growth-*trading* strategies keys on the **rate of change**
of earnings growth (earnings momentum), most often measured **relative to analyst
expectations**. The flagship variant: **buy stocks where analysts are revising
earnings forecasts upward** and ride the price as it follows the revisions
(p.24). US studies find this can earn excess returns, with three refinements:
(1) **bold** revisions that diverge from consensus move prices more and are more
likely accurate than herd-hugging revisions; (2) **timeliness** matters —
earlier revisers have far more price impact than late ones; (3) revisions from
analysts at **larger banks/brokerages** carry more impact (wider reach). Caveats
(p.25): the strategy leans on the **two weakest links** in markets — firm
earnings reports and analyst forecasts; the excess returns likely exist *because*
influential analysts move their clients' trades, but whether the effect is
**lasting** is doubtful; and it is a **short-horizon** play (weeks to a few
months) yielding **small** excess returns that may not survive transaction costs.
Rising market skepticism toward both earnings reports and forecasts "bodes ill"
for it.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.23–25

### 2.13 Determinants of success in growth investing
The chapter closes (p.26 "GARP" chart; p.27) by naming what actually separates
winners from losers when investing in young/growth firms:
- **Better growth estimates** — growth is *the* value driver here; if you follow a
  small set of names and can estimate growth **more precisely than the market**,
  you get paid for it.
- **A long time horizon** — always helpful, but **even more critical** for young/
  growth stocks, because a much larger share of their trading comes from
  **traders playing mood/momentum** than from investors, and traders can push an
  under- or over-valued stock to further extremes short-term. You need the
  staying power to wait for value to assert itself.
- **Macro-forecasting / market-timing skill** — because growth screens run in
  cycles tied to the overall market and economy, the ability to time those cycles
  (earnings or economic growth) can substantially augment returns.
> source: Ch15.pdf p.26–27

## 3. Metrics, formulas & rules of thumb

- **Price per user / EV per subscriber** — the dominant *pricing* metric for
  pre-revenue young companies: company price ÷ number of users (or subscribers,
  downloads). Low = "cheap," high = "expensive." Damodaran's caution: it treats
  all users as equal and ignores user growth, intensity, churn, acquisition cost,
  revenue model and corporate drag — see §6.1. (Ch15.pdf p.3; SSRN 3175652)
- **VC pricing-noise rules of thumb** — small samples + infrequent updating +
  opaque deal terms ⇒ noisier, more subjective, more lagged marks, with feedback-
  driven spirals in both directions. (Ch15.pdf p.4–5)
- **IPO allotment / winner's-curse rule** — in underpriced (oversubscribed) deals
  you get a small allotment; in overpriced deals you get filled — so naïve IPO
  subscription is structurally adverse-selected. (Ch15.pdf p.15)
- **Post-IPO long-run drift** — 3-year post-offering returns ≈ **−5.3% vs market /
  −1.5% style-adjusted** (averages; medians ≈ −6.7% / −3.6%) → new issues
  underperform. (Ch15.pdf p.14)
- **High-P/E penalty** — top-decile-P/E portfolios historically **underperform**
  low-P/E ones (1927–2023), more so value-weighted. (Ch15.pdf p.21)
- **Growth-persistence is low** — period-to-period earnings-growth correlations
  are weak (weakest for large caps) → **past growth ≠ future growth**.
  (Ch15.pdf p.20)
- **PEG ratio** (Peter Lynch; §6.3) — PEG = (P/E) ÷ (expected earnings growth %);
  **< 1.0 attractive, ≤ 0.5 ideal**. **Yield-adjusted PEG** = P/E ÷ (EPS growth +
  dividend yield), used for income-paying slower growers.
- **User lifetime-value identity** (SSRN 3175652):
  `Value of a user-based company = PV(Existing Users) + PV(New Users) − PV(Corporate Drag)`,
  where value of an existing user = PV of after-tax cash flow per user over user
  life, weighted by the annual **renewal rate** (e.g., a 90% renewal rate ⇒ ~43%
  survival probability into year 8 of a 15-year life); value of a **new** user =
  value of existing user **minus cost of acquiring the user**, time-discounted.
- **Earnings-revision refinements** — bolder, earlier, big-broker revisions have
  larger and more reliable price impact; effect is short-horizon (weeks–months)
  and small after costs. (Ch15.pdf p.24–25)

> source: Ch15.pdf p.3–5, p.14–15, p.20–21, p.24–25; SSRN 3175652

## 4. Examples & cases

- **VC pricing ladder** — recent same-company round, "similar" private deals
  (scaled to users/downloads), and public peers, as the three rungs of how a VC
  attaches a price. (Ch15.pdf p.3)
- **Uber (June 2017)** — the chapter's flagship cross-reference into the
  user-valuation paper: 40M riders, ~$20B gross billings, ~$6.5B revenue, $2.8B
  loss; aggregated value ≈ **$36B** vs a rider-by-rider user-based value of
  operating assets ≈ **$31.5B** (≈$42.5B with Didi stake + cash). Existing-user
  value ≈ $449/user; new-user value ≈ $210/user after ~$239 acquisition cost;
  corporate drag ≈ $10.4B. (SSRN 3175652 — see §6.1)
- **Netflix (April 2018)** — value per existing subscriber ≈ $509 (renewal 92.5%,
  cost of capital 7.95%); new subscriber ≈ $398 after $111 acquisition cost; a
  huge **corporate (content) cost drag of ≈ $111B**; equity value/share ≈
  **$172.82** vs a $280 market price → market over-valuing or the analyst too
  pessimistic. (SSRN 3175652)
- **Amazon Prime (April 2018)** — 100M members; existing-member value ≈ $585;
  Prime stand-alone value ≈ **$72.9B**, rising **above $100B** when the
  $99→$119 fee hike is incorporated — illustrates real-option/upside from a
  loyal base. (SSRN 3175652)
- **Spotify (March 2018 pricing)** — naïve EV/user from Netflix ($1,165/user)
  implies an absurd ~$185B for Spotify; the disciplined adjustment for Spotify's
  *variable, user-linked* content cost (subscriber worth ~1/5 of a Netflix
  subscriber) cuts the imputed value to ≈ **$17.7B**. Bulls cited the Netflix
  comp, bears cited Pandora — "they were both wrong." (SSRN 3175652)
- **Facebook** — >2.2B users, >50% operating margin on targeted-ad revenue; the
  archetype of the user/big-data/network-benefit "value star," with high
  per-user value and low acquisition cost. (SSRN 3175652)
- **Blue Apron** — McCarthy's customer-based analysis flagged ~70% of customers
  leaving within six months and the IPO as over-priced — a churn-driven
  cautionary tale. (SSRN 3175652)
- **Peter Lynch's category cases** — McDonald's (a stalwart/fast-grower returning
  ~3,000% over 20 years), and his "invest in what you know" anecdotes — the GARP
  exemplar of public growth investing. (§6.3)
- **Dot-com '90s vs 2001–2008** — the canonical growth-vs-value regime swing
  cited for the cyclicality of growth screens. (§6.5)

> source: Ch15.pdf p.3, p.6–8; SSRN 3175652; §6.1, §6.3, §6.5

## 6. Supplementary readings — distilled

### 6.1 Reading — "Going to Pieces: Valuing Users, Subscribers and Customers" (Damodaran, SSRN 3175652)
*Read in full (SSRN page was 403; full-text PDF obtained via a Colorado-Leeds
mirror and parsed locally).* The paper builds the **disaggregated / user-based**
valuation toolkit that the chapter's pricing critique implicitly demands. Core
identity:
`Value = PV(Existing Users) + PV(New Users) − PV(Corporate Drag)`.
**Existing users:** value = PV of after-tax cash flow per user over the user's
finite life, weighted by **renewal rate** (churn is destiny — a 90% renewal rate
implies only ~43% survival into year 8 of a 15-year life). Cash flow per user is
**revenue per user net of servicing cost and tax** — *not* revenue (a $120
Netflix subscriber spending $30 to service and taxed at 20% yields ~$72 of cash
flow, not $120). **New users:** worth **less** than existing users because of
**acquisition cost** (value of new user = value of existing user − CAC, then
time-discounted); spending too much to acquire users **destroys value** just as
over-paying for growth does in any firm. **Corporate drag:** G&A and other costs
not traceable to users, computed as a residual (total opex − servicing cost −
acquisition cost) and capitalized; high *fixed* drag amplifies both upside and
downside (implosion risk if growth slows). Five "first-principle" traps it warns
against: user immortality, treating revenue as cash flow, "new-user magic"
(free growth), corporate-cost vacuums, and suspending competitive dynamics.
**Value propositions:** (1) losses spent *acquiring* users are worth more than
losses spent *servicing* them; (2) **fixed-cost** models are worth more than
variable-cost ones (economies of scale — why Netflix > Spotify, whose content
cost is variable/user-linked); (3) growth from **revenue-per-existing-user** is
worth more than growth from **new-user count** (Uber moves from worthless to
>$62B as per-user growth rises 0%→20%); (4) the optimal revenue model
(subscription / transaction / advertising) varies with life-cycle stage and
focus; (5) optionality is greatest for firms with **sticky, intense** users in
unpredictable markets (Facebook, Amazon Prime). Worked cases: **Uber** ($31.5B
user-based vs $36B aggregated), **Netflix** (~$173/share vs $280 market),
**Amazon Prime** (~$73B, >$100B post fee-hike), and a **Spotify** pricing
exercise exposing naïve EV/user comparables ($185B absurd → ~$17.7B adjusted).
Adds beyond the slides: the *constructive* framework (the slides critique
price-per-user; the paper shows how to do it properly), the explicit
existing-vs-new-user decomposition, and the user-disclosure checklist firms
should publish. Links to [[cap_10_valuing_young]] and [[cap_11_valuing_high_growth]].
> source: https://ssrn.com/abstract=3175652 (full text via https://leeds-faculty.colorado.edu/bhagat/Valuing-Users.pdf)

### 6.2 Blog — "The Compressed Tech Life Cycle: The Investor Challenge" (Damodaran, Dec 2015)
*Read in full via WebFetch.* Argues that **tech companies age in dog years** —
intense growth (~50%) for ~5 years, a short ~5-year mature plateau (~10% growth),
then rapid decline — versus non-tech firms with gentler ~25% growth and a ~10-year
mature stretch. This compression **breaks classical metrics**: young tech looks
expensive on P/E even when fairly valued, while old tech (>35 years) looks
artificially cheap and trades at much lower multiples than old non-tech. It
indicts three **value-investing** tenets when applied to tech (trusting P/E,
buy-and-hold-forever "good" companies, preferring dividends to buybacks — old
tech rationally returns cash via buybacks) and warns **growth investors** that
PEG can flash "undervalued" precisely when explosive growth is least sustainable.
Data points: tech's S&P 500 weight rose from ~6% (1990) to ~20% (2015); R&D
expensing depresses young-tech reported earnings. Implication for terminal value:
perpetual-growth DCF can over-value declining tech — prefer liquidation value,
finite growing annuities, or negative terminal-growth perpetuities. Adds beyond
the slides: the explicit **time-compression** mechanism and the dividend-vs-
buyback corollary that ties into [[cap_08_cash_return]]. Pairs with the
managerial companion in [[cap_18_managing]].
> source: https://aswathdamodaran.blogspot.com/2015/12/the-compressed-tech-life-cycle-investor.html

### 6.3 Reading — The Peter Lynch approach (CSULB / Validea distillation)
*The CSULB course page (home.csulb.edu/~pammerma/...) 404'd/redirected on direct
fetch; distilled from the Validea "Investment Strategy of Peter Lynch" page plus
the corroborating CSULB-sourced search abstract.* Lynch is the archetypal
**GARP** (growth-at-a-reasonable-price) public growth investor: "invest in what
you know," buy what you understand, and only pay for growth that actually exists.
He sorts companies into **six categories**, each with its own expectations and
exit discipline: **fast growers** (20%+ EPS growth, ideally 20–50%; the
"ten-bagger" hunting ground; he wants sales >$1B and P/E <40), **stalwarts**
(large, 10–20% growth; expect "good not enormous" returns, e.g. ≤50% over two
years, and trim/rotate), **slow growers** (mature, <10% growth, held for
dividends; yield >3% or > S&P average), **cyclicals** (returns track the economic
cycle — buy/sell with the cycle), **turnarounds** (distressed recoveries,
uncorrelated with the market), and **asset plays** (hidden assets the market
ignores). The signature screen is the **PEG ratio** = (P/E) ÷ (expected earnings
growth): **< 1.0 attractive, ≤ 0.5 ideal**; for income-paying names he uses the
**yield-adjusted PEG** = P/E ÷ (EPS growth + dividend yield). Balance-sheet
guards: **debt/equity preferably <50%, avoid >80%**; inventory should not grow
faster than sales; for financials, equity/assets >5% and ROA >1%. He favors
lesser-known, lightly-covered firms and reads insider buying as a positive
signal. Adds beyond the slides: the concrete, screenable embodiment of the
chapter's "buy growth at a reasonable price" branch (Ch15.pdf p.11, p.26) and a
discipline for matching expectations to a company's life-cycle stage —
operationally useful for the copilot's assumption defaults.
> source: https://blog.validea.com/the-investment-strategy-of-peter-lynch/ (CSULB original https://home.csulb.edu/~pammerma/fin382/screener/lynch.htm not directly retrievable — 404 on fetch)

### 6.4 Reading — "How to invest like Peter Lynch" (Motley Fool)
*Read in full via WebFetch (the public Fool "famous-investors/peter-lynch" page).*

### 6.5 Reading — "Growth vs Value Investing" (Forbes — substituted with Schwab/Fidelity/Hartford)
*The Forbes Advisor article was not fetchable (Forbes blocks the agent; HTTP

### 6.6 Data — PitchBook VC / private-market indexes
*Methodology distilled from PitchBook's public report-methodology and
private-market-index pages (the index article itself returned 403).* The indexes
report **pooled, capital-weighted IRRs** built from constituent funds' cash flows
and NAVs, plus **pooled quarterly returns** (aggregate %Δ in NAV adjusted for
contributions/distributions ≈ a quarterly compounded growth rate). Because of
**reporting lag** (late filers, liquidated funds dropping out), recent quarters
carry forward stale multiples/IRRs — the empirical face of Damodaran's claim that
private/VC marks **lag** public markets and are **smoothed** (§2.2). Adds beyond
the slides: the concrete mechanism (NAV-based, infrequently updated fund marks)
behind VC return-series smoothing and the up-/down-market deal-flow cyclicality.

## 7. Takeaways for valuation & modeling

For an equity analyst building standardized models of young/growth firms (raw
material for [[application_to_copilot]]):

1. **Separate pricing from value, and label which one you're doing.** For
   pre-revenue or loss-making names, price-per-user / EV-per-subscriber and
   revenue multiples are *pricing*, not intrinsic value. The copilot must tag any
   such number as a **pricing metric with method + comparables**, never present it
   as estimated intrinsic value (compliance: estimates labeled with method/source,
   never dressed as fact).

2. **When user/subscriber metrics drive the story, model them explicitly.** Build
   the value as `PV(existing users) + PV(new users) − PV(corporate drag)`, with
   per-user **cash flow net of servicing cost and tax** (not revenue), an explicit
   **renewal/churn rate** (the single highest-leverage input for subscription
   models), a **customer-acquisition cost** that makes new users worth *less* than
   existing ones, and a residual **corporate drag** line for untraceable G&A.
   This is the user-economics layer the sector templates should expose.

3. **Cross-check user-based value against a top-down DCF.** Damodaran runs both
   for Uber and reconciles the gap. The copilot should produce **both** an
   aggregated FCFF valuation and (where applicable) a user-based valuation, and
   surface the **delta and its driver inputs** for analyst review rather than
   trusting either alone.

4. **Bake the revenue-vs-earnings and cost-structure dynamics into defaults.**
   Prefer growth from **revenue-per-existing-user** over raw user count (it's
   worth more); treat **fixed-cost** structures as value-accretive (scale) but
   flag **implosion risk** if growth stalls; and choose terminal-value mechanics
   that respect the **compressed tech life cycle** (avoid naïve perpetual growth
   for fast-aging tech — consider finite annuities or low/negative terminal
   growth). Ties to [[cap_11_valuing_high_growth]] and [[cap_13_valuing_declining]].

5. **Treat growth screens and momentum signals as cyclical, not structural.**
   Past growth is a weak predictor (low persistence); high-P/E "growth" baskets
   historically underperform; earnings-revision/momentum edges are short-horizon
   and fragile after costs. If the copilot ever surfaces such signals, frame them
   as **regime-dependent** (earnings cycle / yield-curve conditioned) and never as
   recommendations — consistent with the no-advice compliance rule.

6. **For private/early-stage inputs, widen the uncertainty, don't fake
   precision.** VC marks are noisy, lagged and smoothed; replace point estimates
   with **ranges / scenario inputs** (Damodaran uses Monte Carlo for Uber, median
   user value ~$414 with a $289–$551 inter-decile band). The copilot's bull/base/
   bear scenario machinery is the natural home for this — present a value *range*
   with the inputs that drive it, with disclaimers.

> source: synthesis of Ch15.pdf p.2–27 + SSRN 3175652 (§6.1) + compressed-tech blog (§6.2)
